In a couple of weeks, the Church of Scotland General Assembly will take place. The Assembly this year is sitting over a weekend in order to encourage more working‑age elders to attend. However, for the past 20 years there have been Youth Representatives as part of the Assembly. One of the most common criticisms raised about Youth Representatives at the General Assembly is that they are allowed to speak, contribute, and even move motions, yet they are not allowed to vote. From the perspective of young people, this can feel contradictory. If they are invited to help shape the conversation, why are they excluded from the final decision?

At first glance, it can look like young people are being disadvantaged or even discriminated against. But I want to argue that this is not actually the case. Instead, the situation arises from three structural realities within the Church of Scotland:

1. The General Assembly is made up of representatives from specific bodies, and voting rights follow the body you represent.
2. "Youth” is not a legal category in the Church; only “children” and “adults” exist in law.
3. The route to voting is not through the Youth Representative role, although anyone aged 18 or over *can* vote if they follow the same path as every other potential commissioner.

These three points shape everything that follows.

Representation and Presbyterian Governance

The Church of Scotland operates through a Presbyterian system of governance. This is a representative system: congregations elect elders and ministers who act on their behalf. Elders are ordained for life and share responsibility for the spiritual leadership of the congregation.¹ At the Presbytery level, each Kirk Session nominates one representative elder, and the minister is automatically a member as the teaching elder.² Presbytery meetings are open to all elders, but only those appointed as representatives may speak or vote. At the General Assembly, representation is even more specific. Each charge sends its minister and one elder roughly every four or five years.³ It is a public meeting, so anyone may attend, but commissioners with voting rights attend as representative elders of their Kirk Session, not as individuals. Many people attend the General Assembly — including elders — who do not have a vote.
This is the crucial point: voting rights are tied to the body you represent, not to your age, enthusiasm, or ability. 

 there is a disconnect between membership, baptism, and adulthood. Membership in the Church of Scotland is open to all. The Church predominantly practices infant baptism, meaning children become members of the denomination from the moment they are baptised. This reflects a biblical pattern where faith is lived within families and communities.⁴ However, a tension emerges around age. Confirmation for young teenagers is present conceptually, but largely absent in contemporary Church of Scotland practice. Politically, within Scotland, 16‑year‑olds can vote in national and local elections. In the Church, you cannot be ordained as an elder until you are 18.⁵ This creates a disconnect between civic adulthood and ecclesial adulthood. Young people under 18 can be co‑opted onto working groups of the Kirk Session and may attend Kirk Session meetings, but they must ask permission to speak and cannot vote.

Youth as a Social Imaginary

The Church of Scotland legally recognises only two groups: children (under 18) and adults (18 and over). There is no legal category of “youth.”⁶ This means “youth” is not a fixed category that is consistently applied. It is a social imaginary — something the Church imagines into being, and can re‑imagine at any time. For decades, working with young people meant those registered at parish Bible Class (11–16‑year‑olds). Then in the 1980s–1990s the age range shifted to “young people and students,” meaning 11–25. The Church of Scotland has never formally defined this and has consistently appealed to age‑ranges rather than a theological or legal definition. During the Year of Young People (2018), the definition shifted again to 8–30.⁷ More recently, the Church has spoken about under‑40s. If a category keeps changing, the Church cannot grant representation to something it has not defined.

Since the early 2010s, the Church has increasingly spoken about youth ministry, and since around 2018, about intergenerational ministry — and very recently, under‑40s.⁸ What remains unclear is whether this is meant to be additive (children’s work + youth work + families work = something greater, called intergenerational work) or subtractive (intergenerational ministry replaces specialised youth work or specialised children’s work). It is notable that the Church has not chosen to refill previous children’s worker roles. The “under‑40s” emphasis complicates this further. The risk is that specialised ministries become blurred into one broad demographic category — abstracted into something lesser.

Youth Representatives at the General Assembly are 18–25‑year‑olds, sent by their Presbyteries. They can attend the Assembly, ask questions, move motions, and contribute fully to debate. But they cannot vote.⁹ This is not discrimination. It is simply that Youth Representatives do not represent a recognised voting constituency. They represent “youth,” which, as we have seen, is not a legal or structural category within the Church.

The Path to Voting Rights
This is the part that is often misunderstood.
Currently, young people (18–25) *can* vote at the General Assembly. But the route is not through the Youth Representative constituancy.
To vote, a young person must: - be ordained as an elder (age 18+), and - when the congregation is asked by the Presbytery to nominate an elder to attend Assembly, be nominated by their Kirk Session. This is the same path everyone else follows. There is no separate or discriminatory barrier for young people.!

However, there is a longer and tougher route to voting in the future. The General Assembly franchise already includes people who are not representing parishes — the 5 most recent former Moderators of the General Assembly (if not already commissioners) and members of the Deaconate for example.¹⁰ They are included because they bring a particular wisdom and tenor to the debate. This precedent *could* support the case for giving young people voting rights on the basis of contribution rather than constituency. But for that to happen, the Church would need to define “youth” and create a new representative category grounded in law and governance. It is also worth noting that several other groups who could be included in voting rights are not — such as Assembly Trustees or conveners of committees who are not commissioners. This makes the argument more complex.

The exclusion of Youth Representatives from voting at the General Assembly is not discrimination. It is a structural feature of Presbyterian governance and the absence of a legal category for “youth.” Young people can vote — just not through the Youth Representative role. If the Church wishes to change this, it must first define “youth,” then create a representative structure that reflects that definition. Until then, Youth Representatives remain valued contributors, but not voting commissioners.
