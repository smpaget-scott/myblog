I write this in the Interregnum between Mr And Dr. This in-between time allows me to express gratitude for my academic journey.
My academic journey would not have been possible without my wife Helen and family. My family (and friends) supported and encouraged me to keep doing this. Thank you. I also wish to thank and remember people, including but not limited to those I mention below. However, to everyone involved in my journey, no matter how small. Thank you. I couldn't have done it without you.
- Secondary school
- BAYAT
- PCCE
- Mth
- DPT
**Secondary school**  
Secondary school could have been a better learning experience. I enjoyed it and the friends I made there, but I am not sure I was good at school-based learning. I left with a s [tandard grade 2 in PE, some standard grades at 4 and 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Grade), and no highers I didn't think about attending university, even though my older brother and younger sister did before I did.
During my apprenticeship, I took a higher course in English at night school and got a C from Falkirk College of Technology. (Yes, I am old enough).  
After finishing my apprenticeship, the Unemployment service paid for me to get to college to get some qualifications, and they paid me to learn!  
I earned a Higher Modern Studies at B, based on knowing lots of obscure UN agencies. I failed higher Maths and Physics; perhaps my brain didn't work the way Maths and Physics wanted me to work.
**BAYAT**  
But those two Highers were enough to get in somewhere. At age 21, I applied as a mature student for the BA Youthwork with Applied Theology at International Christian College. I studied youth work and had some classes in theology and a heap of practice. The course was demanding and hard. 30 started in our class, and only eight of my cohort graduated when I did. No one did honours. It was a significant time to be part of a course which placed work with young people in churches as worthy of serious consideration. It asked questions about church work with young people's validity as a practice within work within society generally.
I found a place where I could pass things. I also found a course I could pass without sitting exams. Time spent in youth work practice, reflecting upon that practice, and doing coursework and essays were what was required. I could do that, and I did.  
Thanks, particularly, to Neil Pratt, [Linda Dunnett](https://www.sanctuaryfirst.org.uk/video/watch/linda-dunnet-reads-peter-24-10), and Kenny Wilson for allowing me to be good enough in that academic space. Also, to members of my cohort who participated in this with me: Rev Sarah, Rev Louise, Denise, Mark, Alison, Colin, Aaron, and Chris. Thank you all.
Academically, I was annoyed that the ICC course did not have professional validation. Although it was a full degree, to work for councils, etc., you needed a professional qualification for CEVE. So, I approached my bosses, Sharon and Pam, with the idea that I could do the PCCE course at Strathclyde University and work full-time.
**PCCE**  
I was accepted and graduated with a Postgraduate Certificate in Community Education from Strathclyde University. The course challenged me to get beyond taking things at face value and question myself and what other people were saying or doing.
I remember [Prof Akwugo](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/soc/sociology/staff/akwugoemejulu/) shaking her head vigorously in outrage and frustration at me, "Why would you even say such a thing?". I answered that the person I interviewed said it; it was immediately obvious that my response was unsatisfying. I would take a lot more critical notice of what people said and how I used it.   
It was also affirming; I distinctly remember [Dr Annette](https://research-portal.uws.ac.uk/en/persons/annette-coburn) nodding vigorously during my entrance interview. That was a good sign that I had something to offer. . Thank you to the staff and my cohort for inspiring and challenging me equally, especially Prof Howard, [Prof Akwugo](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/soc/sociology/staff/akwugoemejulu/), Dr Brian, and [Dr Annette](https://research-portal.uws.ac.uk/en/persons/annette-coburn).
Ian became my boss, and he had a Doctorate. Thank you, Ian, for showing a higher academic qualification. It does not mean you must change how you care, laugh, and work for and with people. It isn't a route to fame and fortune. It just means you bring a different set of tools to that job.
**Mth**  
The next period of academic study was at the University of Glasgow, where I did part-time research for a Master's in theology. Due to various life changes, I was no longer an employee of a church but a full-time stay-at-home dad. Studying Practical Theology gave me a way to ask questions, which allowed me to critically examine the religious faith I had grown up with and still have while reflecting on its practice and real life here and now. Thank you, [Dr Doug Gay](https://www.gla.ac.uk/schools/critical/staff/douglasgay/), for your guidance and hard work in making sense of unique spelling. My study of computer games, youth work, and ethics was passable at Masters level with minor changes!
In seeking to talk about how similar the place of church work with young people and council work with young people was significant and helpful in their approach to ethics and computer games, I decided to present at an international conference about youth work.
In one of my early jobs, my boss, Iain, attended a youth work conference at Cambridge University while my colleague and I simultaneously went to work training near Birmingham. On the train back up the road, he gave us both a copy of the papers from that conference, "[IASYM](https://iasym.org)", it was called. (The papers are physical printouts and full of funky fonts). I went to The European Conference of the [International Association for the Study of Youth Ministry](https://iasym.org)(IASYM) without knowing much about it. I presented a paper to five people. I sacred myself. I heard myself talk confidently and knowledgeably about something and was terrified. I was still determining if I could say something to be helpful to others, sensible, and take challenges from academics well. However, it was a tremendously welcoming community where I was cared for and allowed to speak to others who looked the same as me from around the world. Today, I'm very fortunate to be a member of the International Executive Board for the [International Association for the Study of Youth Ministr](https://iasym.org) y (IASYM). The association has significantly challenged my understanding of youth ministry, which may not have happened if I had stayed in Scotland. Thank you to all the academics and researchers who made it so.
**DPT**  
I had never dreamed I could do the process of a doctoral study. However, I embarked on this study knowing I had done the Mth process reasonably well and with the encouragement of others, including people from within the [IASYM](https://iasym.org). It was a scary journey. It was helpful to meet with a cohort and encourage each other onward. Thanks to David, Stephen, Sheena, Alistair, and others in cohorts of other years who encouraged and listened to me. There was an openness that embodied an attitude that we don't always agree with. But we understand what you're saying and encourage you to look at this further. I was glad of their company and challenege.
In particular, the course staff, [Prof Heather](https://www.gla.ac.uk/schools/critical/staff/heatherwalton/) and [Dr Doug](https://www.gla.ac.uk/schools/critical/staff/douglasgay/) gave so much without knowing it. There was a spirit of generosity, and each student was taken seriously while also allowing people to challenge each other well. The course allowed me to do a doctorate that I never thought I could do, I still don't think I can do, and I am still amazed that I even attempted it.
My primary advisor has been [Dr Doug Gay](https://www.gla.ac.uk/schools/critical/staff/douglasgay/) through my MTh and Doctorate. He has been hospitable, welcoming and encouraging on this whole journey for the last 12 years. It didn't matter how I felt (primarily nervous and unprepared). I always left supervision with a feeling I could do this. I had something to say and felt like I could do this. That's amazing. I didn't know I should think of more and do more. So thank you. Your friendship and wisdom mean a lot to me.
**So what now?**  
It's interesting to look back and think about the people I went to university with over the four times I've done it.  
Some are ministers.  
Some are youth workers.  
Some are surprised I have not ended up in jail so far.  
Some have changed jobs significantly; some need to be more academics involved in training the youth workers and theologians.
As for me, let's see what opportunities open up for me to use the new tools I have to work well. In the meantime, [I Appreciate'cha](https://youtu.be/ENjZ0kN0BRA?feature=shared)
