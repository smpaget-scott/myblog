#### TL-DR
The Presbytery of Forth Valley and Clydesdale (my employer) will publish a draft of the Presbytery Mission Plan in the next few weeks. It outlines a mission plan for the entire Presbytery (strategy and implementation timetable) over the next five years. As the Presbytery's mission officer, I have not received a copy of the Presbytery Mission Plan.  
I look forward to being surprised and, no doubt, delighted by this work when I read it.
As I read the draft, I have three questions in my mind. The questions are.  
 1—What is the strategy for the Presbytery's mission? (How is the mission distributed across the Code of Practice and 5 Marks of Mission?)   
 2—What is the strategic approach to developing new worshipping communities and working with under-40s within the Presbytery? (How have these opportunities been strategically identified? How are they resourced?).  
 3—How are people treated? (What language does the draft use?)
##### Long Post warning - This post is long and will take a few moments to read.
**Context**  
The Church of Scotland (CofS) decided it could not continue as it was currently structured. After an attempt at reform at the General Assembly (GA) 2018, it recommended structural change at the GA 2019. The CofS decided to change the way it runs nationally (six central committees (CofA, M&D, MC, C&S, WM, GT) down to four committees (FNF, FAF, AT and GT, subsequently at GA2023, three central committees (AT, FAPLT, GT) \*see note 1. It voted for fewer but larger regional presbyteries (from 42 to 12). It also voted that the same rationalisation should occur with local congregations. As every congregation was an independent charity, this would be quite a feat (from over 1000 different charities).
How to do this was the responsibility of each regional body.
**Background**  
The Presbytery of Forth Valley and Clydesdale (my employers) tried to plan for a Presbytery Mission Plan (PMP) through 2021-2022. _I was part of the Mission Planning Team (MPT) until I resigned in June 2022 to work as an employee for the Presbytery._ The MPT made a critical error when I was on the team. The decision that this work could be done without visiting every Kirk Session as part of this work was a mistake. I apologise as part of my corporate responsibility. After eleven months of hard work, the first team resigned without presenting a draft.
The second Mission Plan Action Group (MPAG) was constituted in October 2022. It oversaw a process where congregations found partner congregations to discuss commonalities and plan the mission for them in their context(s). Once a plan is agreed upon locally, the MPAG tried to honour this agreement. This process created a draft which was presented to Presbytery in September 2023. That draft was not rejected but paused. As part of this pause, the MPAG team was asked to comment on the precise rationale for changes, where the draft PMP had changed what the Kirk session had discussed and agreed on themselves. The convenor of the MPAG resigned. However, the MPAG members decided to continue on. _As a presbytery worker, I supported the group in their work._
The remaining group formulated a precise rationale, and the team changed the draft outcomes for some congregations before representing a draft PMP to the Presbytery. The group decided that they did not want or need support from Presbytery workers in this part of their work. _As such, I have not been involved in their work since September 2023._
This post allows me to suggest some principles and questions for this document to guide my reading and interaction with the draft PMP. It will help me clarify what I am looking for and what I hope to see.
**Principle one**
- To be a Presbytery Mission Plan, it should focus strategically on what the mission should look like across the Presbytery until 2027. It should be prophetic, visionary and bold. The Draft PMP should identify the mission across the Presbytery and then deal with what resources are needed to back that vision contextually (staffing and buildings). \*see note 1. There could be an approach where the draft PMP will include staffing not as a resource for the mission but as a critical part of the mission; however, this is dangerous. This approach seeks to build mission thinking upon an incumbent; it is unfair to associate that church performance with an individual/team, not the congregation/membership. It speaks of mission as something someone else (minister) does for us (congregation). That's not fair.
_The draft PMP should have a strategic missional outline for the whole Presbytery to work on together._
**Principle two**
- The Mission Plan is centred on the 5 Marks of Mission adopted and developed by the Anglican communion. These are great; however, they are not suitable for planning. \*See Note 2. One of the central committees of the CofS has given us a Code of Practice for use in creating the Presbytery Mission Plan \*see note 3. The idea is that for each Code of Practice area, the 5 Marks of Mission ask questions about what is planned.
_The draft PMP should have a strategic approach to the eight categories of the Code of Practice and show how the 5 Marks of Mission interact with them._
**Principle three**
- The Church of Scotland has made Mission with Under 40s and the development of new worship communities a priority nationally, and this priority is seen with increased resources. The Draft PMP should strategically engage with this national priority, showing how it is also a priority for the Presbytery (which is a lower court). Of course, the group may decide these are not priorities for the Presbytery. The mission plan will have to say something definitive about these.
_The draft PMP should clearly strategise around national priorities (or if not, argue why not)._
**Principle four**
One of the central questions that drove this time of change within the CofS was, "Does the CofS ask too much and give too little" to people? One way this was worked out in Forth Valley and Clydesdale Presbytery's initial structure was to ask, "Are we spending too much time in committees and not enough on accompanying each other in mission?"
In the Church of Scotland, we fetishise committees. You know the joke: how many members of the Church of Scotland does it take to change a lightbulb? There are many variations of an answer. However, all start with the line - "first, you form a committee". As members of the CofS, the story we tell ourselves is that we form committees. We poke fun at this but also find comfort in our ritualistic response. This action of creating a committee gives comfort and a space to thrive. The current presbytery structure was very small. It has three main committees (similar to the position of the National Church of Scotland), and there was mistrust, fears about misuse of power, and discomfort about what people were not being told. Therefore, a new structure of eight new committees is proposed. There will also be over 100 voluntary positions within the Presbytery. This might be the right move to create a space of trust, power, equality, comfort and thriving.
The principle of the mission plan should be that it asks less of people and gives more. The draft PMP should explicitly explain how the MPAG strategic vision should treat people. What people will need and how this can be achieved. One of the key ways this lightness can be found will be in the language used about adjustments. Plain and straightforward, hopeful and realistic, giving comfort and joy.
_The mission plan should treat people as fragile beings using clear and plain language to outline the mission future of transformation and new social possibilities for each congregation._
Based on these four principles, I have three questions that will inform my reading of the draft PMP.
**1—** What is the strategy for the Presbytery's mission until 2027? (How is the mission distributed across the Code of Practice and 5 Marks of Mission? what does the future CofS look like in 2027?)
**2—** What is the strategic approach to developing new worshipping communities and working with under-40s within the Presbytery? (How have these opportunities been strategically identified? How are they resourced?).
**3—** How are people treated? (What language does the draft use? How are people involved in the process? How is hope given within the change?)
The three questions above give me a grounding and a foothold in the document. They allow me to notice the prophetic, visionary and bold nature of the draft PMP. How and where is it prophetic? How does it signify where the Presbytery should deal with loss and anger? Where should the Presbytery find importance in worship? Where is the Presbytery resourcing a transformation and speaking of a new future?
This is inspired by the Old Testament prophets and their work within a culture of despair, denial, dislocation and, on some occasions, Exile. Scholar Walter Brueggemann argues that the church can speak with a voice that has four identifiable voices within it. A voice of Sadness, Rage, Anger and Loss. It needs to talk in a voice that calls people back to Holiness, proper worship, and sabbath. The church needs to speak inspiringly of imaginative, neighbourly transformation. Finally, the church needs to create new social possibilities by showing and acting by speaking the news of God.
I think the draft PMP must do this to resonate with people in congregations and communities. I hope it is prophetic, visionary and bold. I hope to be surprised and delighted by it.
