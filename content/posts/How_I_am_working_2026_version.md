1.    COUNTDOWN  Widget. 

The Countdown app occupies the top-left area and covers lines 1 and 2. Currently, it is showing the time to the next syndicate meeting.  This is a great little app. It just counts down to the next major event you need to remember. The app shows you a live countdown to the event on your homescreen. The app imports events directly from the phone calendar and displays them in this countdown format. The app relies on you entering the events into your calendar. 

There's one problem with this app: it starts counting up after you have passed the event, but there's no indicator to let you know it's counting up. But if you're looking at an event every day, you know roughly where you are,

Pokémon Go allows you to play the game. I do play the game, and I'm quite okay at it. I like Pokémon Go and will try to catch 'em all. 3.    Halide widget

At the bottom left of the screen, there's a widget to open Halide. Halide is a phone app that may be more capable than the stock camera app. In anything, you make choices, and they become the defaults for the way you do something. The iOS camera and software make several choices for photography and image processing. Halide makes different choices with the same hardware. It uses iPhone camera technology in a way different from a typical iOS phone, giving you much more control. I really like it. The app's one downside is that it doesn't have automatic barcode recognition. Still, it saves all your pictures in raw formats, including the process-zero raw mode that stops iOS software from processing the raw image! This is a different approach to the usual iPhone standard image.

In the top row, middle-right, there is the default calendar app. The app is one of the few apps on iOS that is dynamic and changes every day.  It sits on the top row in the a space of very little interaction, but it displays the current date.

On the third row, in the middle-right, we have the Google Maps icon. I used to use Waze. Waze was a more driving, centred app. There. 8.    Otter

On the third row, in the middle-right, we find the Otter app. Otter AI is a service that lets you record voice notes and transcribe them to text. It automatically transcribes, but word-for-word, sound-for-sound. So it includes where you have bad syntax, it keeps your bad syntax in the translation, as accuracy is key. And from that point of view, when I'm doing any writing, it's really useful to be able to say that's my own voice. That's the way I speak. I then decide to clean up the transcription. If I do, I can then decide what level of cleaning I do, whereas if I had to go through a different model that summarises and cleans up your text, it would be unsatisfying, as I could not be sure it is not my own work or thought. While it tries to get me to do the AI summary thing in Otter, the core product I use it for is a word-for-word, sound-for-sound translation of what I'm saying. 

*** While I have enjoyed using this app, some issues around app security, Data protection, GDPR, and privacy have been passed to me. I am working out what alternatives may be to address these concerns. ***

10.    Tapestry

Long ago, in the olden days of the Internet, there was a standard called RSS (Really Simple Syndication). RSS is a standard web technology that provides a feed of your content to anyone on the Internet, allowing users and applications to keep track of updates to websites in a standardised form. It is very simple and very good. By subscribing to RSS feeds, you can follow many websites as they update or publish new articles without having to visit each website. If something was on the web, you could follow and be notified when it changed. 

So it was between 2005 and 2006 when RSS first came on my radar, and it also became the main way to check the Internet; as the RSS reader regularly checks the websites you want to read for you, downloads any updates, and notifies you. 

You could subscribe to your email client and use RSS like email. Or you could use specific apps. I used to use various readers, the best of which was the free Google Reader. The problem with a free Google product is that it has to be so big that it becomes part of its core offering. In 2013, presumably not enough people were using Google Reader, and Google closed down its RSS service. 

I don't think RSS is dead. 
RSS gives you more control over what you read and who you want to hear from, and less control to an algorithm that says you will be interested in this, or watch this advert for something you did a web search for 3 days ago. RSS's core offering is to give you what you ask for—no more, no less. Notably, Facebook and the app formerly known as Twitter have stopped RSS feeds. 

In the days when social media is becoming less about what you want to follow. And more about "oh, we think you will like these things" based on advertising and keeping you interested, so you make us money. RSS is a relaxing space where you can read the things you want. So anyway, I'm a big fan of the Tapestry app. 

The Icon Factory creates the Tapestry app. If you don't know the Icon Factory, it provided Twitteriffic, possibly the best Twitter client, before Mr Musk killed off 3rd-party app access to the former Twitter service. The Icon Factory has several really interesting apps that let you do a whole bunch of stuff. 

The Tapestry app takes in all your RSS feeds and brings them to you. RSS feeds are on most websites, though they don't advertise them (except Facebook and Twitter). The app has a feed finder box that lets you enter any web address. The app will check whether the website has an RSS feed for you to access. If so, we will add that website to your tapestry timeline. 70% of the websites I tested have RSS feeds for following. A minority of sites charge some fees but give you headlines for free (like The Verge, which gives you the headlines for free, and then the paywall is below the headline).  

The app also allows you to follow Mastodon accounts/ hashtags, Bluesky, Tumblr micro.blog, and Reddit. The crowning glory of Tapestry is a unified timeline. The unified timeline shows all feed entries in time order. Other apps also have this feature, but it's often hidden away in settings (looking at you, Facebook). Here, the app also remembers when you last opened it, and all the new stuff is above you, so you can read and complete your Internet, while older stuff is behind you. It is very natural and intuitive.

13.     JustWatch

In the fifth row, in the middle-right, you have the Just Watch app. Just watch is really simple, a really simple idea. You tell it to film, tell it what services you have, what subscriptions and free things like the iPlayer,  ITV-X, Channel Four webservice, and it tells you, if you can watch that film, and where it is, how much it will be, if you have to buy it, or if you're subscribed to something which gives you it for free. You can also track things. So when there's a movie out, and I like it, I can track it and see where to watch it. If I have access to a streaming service, I can see which platform it's on. The app works really well for film and television. It would be really good if they extended the functionality to books. I assume books were just a different database. They could do that.
