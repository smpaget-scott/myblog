(This is part of my ongoing doctoral studies.)
Andrew Root has written the first of three books on Charles Taylor’s A Secular Age, (hence the Volume one). The book deals with authenticy, it follows Taylors books then moves beyond Taylor to the space our culture needs to hear. Root sees the world which has fetishised youth to its current position within society as a byproduct of its search for authenticity. This search is the affect of a society described by Taylor, where was have lost the immanent frame and are searching for that thing. In page 13-4 Root identifies his project as not a part of Faith Formation with youth ministry, seeing Faith Formation as a mechanism which is actually to blame for MTD (moral therapeutic deism) in youth work of the church.
Root follows a history of the USA out of which he draws how youthfulness has found its central place within american culture, this is important to the emphasis on authenticy. This was explored in detail and the answers which aren’t easy, benefit from an opening half of the book which seems like it takes a long time. When I was reading Taylor’s book, I was struck by the length of the book, and the depth with which the books takes each of it subjects. It is fair to criticise Taylor’s books for being too long. It is worth noting that Root has followed deliberately Taylors move for length and pacing. The book comes in at +200 pages and of the notes, written for myself the central question is why is it taking so long for Root to get there. Root would say stop and listen. His argument in the second half of the book benefits from the time taking in the first half the book. The setting out why the USA was the way it was, the answering of what got us in this state is important to the emphasis on authenticy.
_Point 1: Andrew Root paces the book like Charles Taylor’s._ _Point 2: European vs US_
Coming from an European background to this book gave some pause for reflection. Root was writing an explanation of the way things were for a knowingly American audience. This embodiment of USA as a writing place and a reading place is appropriate as Taylors books refers mostly to the US situation. But Roots book leaves me lukewarm. I am excited and motived by his vision of youth ministry, but his embodiedness leave me distanced by the book and the project. Theologically embodiedness is the right move which speaks openly of the incarnation and Rots locatedness in the USA. This to my Scottish mind feels like a different detached world beyond my recognition. Root is often writing for a wider audience than I provide. I did know most of the history Root provides but i can see is helpful for those who have not a knowledge of American history. It gave rise to another question, who is this book for, at times I thought is was people studying youth ministry to doctoral level, but I often get the feeling that the writing was aimed at those switched on within churches, for example its developed sense of US history feeding into the second more theological part of the book. Its resistance of easy answers in deference for theological principals. I agree with root on his views on youth ministry and where it has to go. Is seems like a linear line can be drawn from The Revisiting Relational youth ministry.
